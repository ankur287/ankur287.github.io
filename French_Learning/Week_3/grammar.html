Articles & Genre

Every French noun has a gender. The article you choose must match it.

1. Articles définis — "The"

Definite articles refer to a specific noun — one you and your listener both know about. They must agree in gender and number with the noun.

le

Masculin singulier

Used before masculine singular nouns that begin with a consonant.

Le garçon mange une pomme.
luh gar-SOHN mahnh ün POM
The boy eats an apple.
Le bureau est grand.
luh bü-ROH eh GRAHN
The office is big.
la

Féminin singulier

Used before feminine singular nouns that begin with a consonant.

La femme lit un livre.
la FAM lee AN lee-vruh
The woman reads a book.
La voiture est rouge.
la vwa-TUR eh ROOZH
The car is red.
l'

Élision — masculin ou féminin

le and la both shorten to l' before a vowel or silent h. Gender is still present — hidden inside the noun itself.

L'homme est grand. (masculin)
LOM eh GRAHN
The man is tall.
L'école est loin. (féminin)
lay-KOL eh LWAN
The school is far.
L'eau est froide. (féminin)
LOH eh FRWAHD
The water is cold.
French avoids two vowel sounds running together (called hiatus). When le or la ends in a vowel and the following word also starts with a vowel, the article vowel is dropped and replaced with an apostrophe: le + école → l'école. This is mandatory — you cannot say le école in standard French. Silent h (like in l'homme) behaves like a vowel for this purpose.
les

Pluriel — masculin & féminin

les is the plural "the" for ALL genders. The noun usually adds -s (silent in speech).

Les enfants jouent dans le jardin.
lay-zan-FAHN zhoo dahn luh zhar-DAN  🔗 les enfants → /lay-z/ liaison
The children play in the garden.
Les livres sont sur la table.
lay lee-vruh sohn sür la TA-bluh
The books are on the table.
When les is followed by a word starting with a vowel or silent h, the normally silent -s is pronounced as /z/ and links to the next word. This is called liaison obligatoire (mandatory liaison). Example: les enfants → /lay-z-ahn-FAHN/. You must always make this liaison — omitting it sounds unnatural in French.
2. Articles indéfinis — "A / An / Some"

Indefinite articles refer to a non-specific noun — something new being introduced, or one among many.

un

Masculin singulier — "a / an"

One masculine thing, not specifically identified.

Je veux un café, s'il vous plaît.
zhuh vuh AN ka-FAY, seel voo PLEH
I want a coffee, please.
C'est un beau pays.
seh AN boh pay-EE
It is a beautiful country.
une

Féminin singulier — "a / an"

One feminine thing, not specifically identified.

Elle a une belle voiture.
el ah ün BEL vwa-TUR
She has a beautiful car.
Je cherche une clé.
zhuh SHAYRSH ün KLAY
I am looking for a key.
des

Pluriel — "some / any"

des is the plural of both un and une. English often omits it, but French requires it.

Il y a des fleurs dans le jardin.
eel ee ah day FLUR dahn luh zhar-DAN
There are (some) flowers in the garden.
Je mange des pommes chaque matin.
zhuh MAHNH day POM shak ma-TAN
I eat (some) apples every morning.
In English you can say "I eat apples" — the plural noun stands alone. In French, you almost always need an article: "Je mange des pommes." The word des tells your listener the noun is plural and indefinite (unspecified apples, not a specific basket). Omitting it sounds telegraphic and unnatural in everyday speech.
3. Masculine vs Feminine — Pattern Tips

There is no perfect rule, but word endings give strong clues. Always learn new nouns with their articles — that's the most reliable strategy.

Usually Masculine

  • -age le fromage
  • -ment l'appartement
  • -eau le bureau
  • -isme le tourisme
  • -in le matin

Usually Feminine

  • -tion la question
  • -té la liberté
  • -eur la couleur*
  • -ure la voiture
  • -ette la fenêtre

* Professions ending in -eur are often masculine: le professeur

4. Phrases de pratique
Phrase 1
La fille cherche un stylo sur la table.
la FEE-yuh SHAYRSH AN stee-LOH sür la TA-bluh
The girl is looking for a pen on the table.
Phrase 2
Les enfants jouent avec des jouets.
lay-zan-FAHN zhoo a-VEK day zhoo-EH  🔗 les enfants → /z/ liaison
The children play with (some) toys.
Phrase 3
L'eau et le pain sont sur la table.
LOH ay luh PAN sohn sür la TA-bluh
The water and the bread are on the table.
Phrase 4
Le monsieur lit un journal dans le café.
luh muh-SYUH lee AN zhoor-NAL dahn luh ka-FAY
The man reads a newspaper in the café.
Phrase 5
Une amie m'apporte des fleurs et un livre.
ün na-MEE ma-PORT day FLUR ay AN lee-vruh  🔗 une amie → /ün-na-MEE/
A (female) friend brings me some flowers and a book.
