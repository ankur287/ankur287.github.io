Articles & Genre

French nouns are either masculine or feminine. The article must always match the noun. This page explains all 7 articles and how to use them.

Le point essentiel

In English, the word "the" works for every noun regardless of gender. In French, there are four words for "the" — and you must pick the right one every time. The same is true for "a". This is why you always learn a noun together with its article: not just livre, but le livre.

Défini ("the") le la les

Indéfini ("a") un une des
1. Articles définis — "The"

Use definite articles when both you and your listener know which specific thing you mean. They agree with the noun in gender and number.

le

Masculin singulier — "the"

Before a masculine singular noun starting with a consonant.

Le garçon est dans le parc.
luh gar-SOHN eh dahn luh PARK
The boy is in the park.
Le matin est calme ici.
luh ma-TAN eh KALM ee-SEE
The morning is quiet here.
la

Féminin singulier — "the"

Before a feminine singular noun starting with a consonant.

La maison est grande.
la may-ZOHN eh GRANHD
The house is big.
La porte est fermée.
la PORT eh fair-MAY
The door is closed.
l'

Élision — masculin ou féminin

Both le and la shorten to l' before a vowel or silent h. The gender of the noun does not change — it is hidden in the noun itself.

L'homme est grand. (masculin)
LOM eh GRAHN
The man is tall.
L'école est loin. (féminin)
lay-KOL eh LWAN
The school is far.
L'eau est froide. (féminin)
LOH eh FRWAHD
The water is cold.
French avoids two vowel sounds running together. When le or la ends in a vowel and the next word starts with one, the article vowel is dropped and replaced by an apostrophe: le + école → l'école. This is not optional — saying le école is a mistake in standard French. Silent h (like in l'homme) behaves like a vowel for this rule.
les

Pluriel — "the" (all genders)

Les is the plural of both le and la. The noun usually adds -s (silent in speech).

Les enfants jouent dans le parc.
lay-zan-FAHN zhoo dahn luh PARK 🔗 les + enfants → /z/ liaison
The children play in the park.
Les maisons sont grandes.
lay may-ZOHN sohn GRANHD
The houses are big.
When les is followed by a word starting with a vowel or silent h, the normally silent final -s is pronounced as /z/ and links to the next word. This is mandatory liaison — omitting it sounds wrong to a native speaker. Example: les enfants → /lay-z-ahn-FAHN/.
2. Articles indéfinis — "A / An / Some"

Use indefinite articles when you are introducing something new, or referring to one thing among many — something unspecific.

un

Masculin singulier — "a / an"

One masculine noun — not a specific one.

Je veux un café, s'il vous plaît.
zhuh vuh AN ka-FAY, seel voo PLEH
I want a coffee, please.
C'est un beau jardin.
seh AN boh zhar-DAN
It is a beautiful garden.
une

Féminin singulier — "a / an"

One feminine noun — not a specific one.

Il y a une école dans ma rue.
eel ee ah ün nay-KOL dahn ma RÜ
There is a school on my street.
Elle a une clé de rechange.
el ah ün KLAY duh ruh-SHAHNH
She has a spare key.
des

Pluriel — "some / any"

The plural of both un and une. English often drops it; French requires it.

Il y a des fleurs dans le jardin.
eel ee ah day FLUR dahn luh zhar-DAN
There are (some) flowers in the garden.
Elle achète des livres chaque mois.
el a-SHET day LEE-vruh shak MWAH
She buys (some) books every month.
In English you can say "I eat apples" — the plural noun stands alone. French almost always requires an article: "Je mange des pommes." The word des tells the listener the noun is plural and unspecified. Omitting it sounds telegraphic and unnatural in everyday French.
3. Comment deviner le genre — Patterns utiles

There is no perfect rule — gender must be memorised with each noun. But word endings give strong clues. Always learn nouns with their article: not just ville but la ville.

Usually Masculine

  • -age  le fromage
  • -eau  le bureau
  • -ment  le moment
  • -in  le matin
  • -isme  le tourisme

Usually Feminine

  • -tion  la question
  • -té  la liberté
  • -ure  la voiture
  • -ée  la journée
  • -ance  la chance
4. Mots connecteurs — Connector words

The reading passage this week uses these connecting words. They appear with nouns and articles you now know. You don't need to master them yet — just recognise them.

Français English Example
il y a there is / there are Il y a une école ici.
dans in / inside dans le jardin
sur on / on top of sur la table
parce que because J'aime ça parce que c'est bon.
près de near / close to près de l'école
Prepositions like dans, sur, près de, avec are invariable — they never change their form regardless of gender or number. Only articles, adjectives, and some pronouns must agree with the noun's gender. Prepositions are safe!
5. Phrases de pratique

Each sentence uses only vocabulary from Weeks 1–3. The blue and pink highlights are always the article.

Phrase 1
La femme est dans le bureau.
la FAM eh dahn luh bü-ROH
The woman is in the office.
Phrase 2
Il y a une école et un parc dans ce quartier.
eel ee ah ün nay-KOL ay AN PARK dahn suh kar-TYAY 🔗 une école → /ün-nay-KOL/
There is a school and a park in this neighbourhood.
Phrase 3
Les garçons font du sport le mardi.
lay gar-SOHN fohn dü SPOR luh mar-DEE
The boys do sport on Tuesday.
Phrase 4
J'ai un ami qui habite près de l'école.
zhay AN na-MEE kee a-BEET preh duh lay-KOL 🔗 un ami → /AN-na-MEE/
I have a friend who lives near the school.
Phrase 5
Il fait du pain et du fromage pour le repas.
eel feh dü PAN ay dü froh-MAZH poor luh ruh-PAH
He makes (some) bread and (some) cheese for the meal.
